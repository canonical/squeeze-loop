CLAIM CARDS

- [verbatim] "e) Separation of privilege: Where feasible, a protection mechanism that requires two keys to unlock it is more robust and flexible than one that allows access to the presenter of only a single key. The relevance of this observation to computer systems was pointed out by R. Needham in 1973. The reason is that, once the mechanism is locked, the two keys can be physically separated and distinct programs, organizations, or individuals made responsible for them. From then on, no single accident, deception, or breach of trust is sufficient to compromise the protected information. This principle is often used in bank safe-deposit boxes. It is also at work in the defense system that fires a nuclear weapon only if two different people both give the correct command. In a computer system, separated keys apply to any situation in which two or more conditions must be met before access should be permitted. For example, systems providing user-extendible protected data types usually depend on separation of privilege for their implementation." (Section I.A.3 "Design Principles", item e). METHOD (three sentences)
This is an invited tutorial/survey paper, not an empirical study; it synthesizes the state of the art in computer information protection circa 1975 across three sections (basic principles, descriptor-based architectures, state of the art). The design principles are presented as distilled engineering heuristics drawn from accumulated experience and prior work (e.g., crediting E. Glaser 1965 for fail-safe defaults and R. Needham 1973 for the computer-systems relevance of separation of privilege), explicitly described as "warnings" rather than absolute rules. No experiments, datasets, or quantitative evaluation are involved.

LIMITATIONS
The principles are normative heuristics, not validated results; the authors themselves caution "these principles do not represent absolute rules--they serve best as warnings." As a 1975 survey, examples and architecture details are dated, though the eight principles have proven durable.
