METHOD (three sentences)
The authors test GPT-4, GPT-3.5 Turbo, and Llama-2-7b-chat as evaluators on text summarization using XSUM and CNN/DailyMail (1,000 articles each), measuring both self-recognition (can the model tell its own summary apart) and self-preference (does it score its own summary higher) in pairwise and individual (Likert 1-5) settings. To establish causality, they use supervised fine-tuning on ~500 pairwise examples to deliberately raise or lower a model's self-recognition ability and observe how self-preference moves in step. They add control fine-tuning tasks (length, vowel count, readability) to confirm the linear trend is not an artifact of fine-tuning per se.
LIMITATIONS (authors' own)
The authors state (§5.2) their experiments provide evidence toward but do not fully validate the causal hypothesis; they did not control for generation quality with ground-truth annotation; they study only text summarization (limited task/dataset/model scope) and used a single prompt design with acknowledged variance, deferring broader validation to future work.
CONTRIBUTION (why this paper is cited here; which manuscript claim it supports; verdict)
Cited to support the manuscript claim that "LLM evaluators recognize their own generations and that self-recognition capability correlates linearly with the strength of self-preference bias." The body confirms both halves verbatim: self-recognition is demonstrated, and the correlation is genuinely described as LINEAR (in-text, Finding, and Figure 1 caption), established via controlled fine-tuning manipulation plus control tasks. VERDICT: SUPPORTED. The flat manuscript phrasing is fair; if anything it understates the method by omitting that the linearity emerges from active fine-tuning manipulation rather than passive observation.
