CLAIM CARDS

1. Quote: "we lay out a set of principles that when followed by a formal method give rise to its mature applicability" (Abstract) -> Paraphrase: the paper is a position/manifesto laying out principles for when a formal method is "applicable", not an empirical study.

4. Quote: success-story evidence "is still insufficient to underpin a strong argument for a wider deployment" and data from a "representative range of samples has hardly ever been rigorously measured (e.g., using controlled method experiments)" (framing) -> Paraphrase: the manifesto itself concedes the field lacks powered, representative evaluation — context for our explicitly-not-powered, existence-demonstration stance.

METHOD
A manifesto / position paper. It organizes applicability into ten principles (Table 1: Scope, Methodology, Integration, Explainability, Automation, Scalability, Transfer, Usefulness, Ease of Use, Evaluation), each explained in turn, and discusses empirical research methods for FM (case studies, action research, controlled method experiments), citing Yin and Wohlin et al. for procedure.

LIMITATIONS / FRAMING (authors' own)
Explicitly not an empirical evaluation; it argues normatively for what applicable FM should satisfy, and concedes that existing applicability evidence is success-story-based and under-measured.

CONTRIBUTION (why cited here)
Cited in §2.6 to position the paper's evaluation against the manifesto's Evaluation + case-study principles and to state partial conformance: the lace application is a "case study" in the manifesto's sense (intensive single example, aim to generalise); the four seeded instances are "existence demonstrations" (intensive but constructed), and a powered, representative comparison is explicitly future work — matching the manifesto's own concession that such measurement is rare. VERDICT: SUPPORTED (partial-conformance framing is faithful to the manifesto's principles).
