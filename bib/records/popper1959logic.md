CLAIM CARDS

1. Theories are tested deductively and a positive result only TEMPORARILY supports them; they are never established as true. [verbatim, Ch.1 §3]
   Quote: "If this decision is positive, that is, if the singular conclusions turn out to be acceptable, or verified, then the theory has, for the time being, passed its test: we have found no reason to discard it. But if the decision is negative, or in other words, if the conclusions have been falsified, then their falsification also falsifies the theory from which they were logically deduced. It should be noticed that a positive decision can only temporarily support the theory, for subsequent negative decisions may always overthrow it."
   Anchor: Chapter 1, §3 "Deductive Testing of Theories" (Routledge Classics 2005, p. 10; same text as 1959 Hutchinson 1st English ed.).
   Paraphrase: Tests can only ever fail to refute a theory; passing a test gives no proof of truth, since a later test may still overthrow it. This is the asymmetry at the core of falsificationism.

2. Popper's own term "corroboration": a theory that withstands severe tests is "corroborated", explicitly NOT verified or proven true/probable. [verbatim, Ch.1 §3]
   Quote: "So long as theory withstands detailed and severe tests and is not superseded by another theory in the course of scientific progress, we may say that it has 'proved its mettle' or that it is 'corroborated' by past experience." ... "I never assume that we can argue from the truth of singular statements to the truth of theories. I never assume that by force of 'verified' conclusions, theories can be established as 'true', or even as merely 'probable'."
   Anchor: Chapter 1, §3 (Routledge Classics 2005, p. 10). Popper's footnote on "corroborated" cross-references the note before §79 and §*29 of his Postscript.
   Paraphrase: "Corroboration" is the trust a theory earns purely by surviving serious attempts to refute it; it is never the same as verification, truth, or even probability. This is exactly the manuscript's claim that trust/corroboration is EARNED BY SURVIVING REFUTATION.

3. Demarcation criterion is falsifiability, NOT verifiability. [verbatim, Ch.1 §6]
   Quote: "These considerations suggest that not the verifiability but the falsifiability of a system is to be taken as a criterion of demarcation." And: "I shall certainly admit a system as empirical or scientific only if it is capable of being tested by experience." And: "I shall not require of a scientific system that it shall be capable of being singled out, once and for all, in a positive sense; but I shall require that its logical form shall be such that it can be singled out, by means of empirical tests, in a negative sense: it must be possible for an empirical scientific system to be refuted by experience."
   Anchor: Chapter 1, §6 "Falsifiability as a Criterion of Demarcation". (Quoted text confirmed against reliable Popper-quote sources and SEP; not re-read from the Ch.1 OCR file, which truncated just before §6 — hence [verbatim] wording but flagged below under Limitations as not personally re-verified against the page image.)
   Paraphrase: A theory is scientific not because experience can confirm it but because experience could in principle refute it; refutability is the line between science and non-science.

4. The logical asymmetry between verification and falsification (Hume's point). [secondary, SEP]
   Quote (SEP): "it is logically impossible to verify a universal proposition by reference to experience (as Hume saw clearly), but a single genuine counter-instance falsifies the corresponding universal law."
   And SEP: "a theory that has withstood rigorous testing should be deemed to have received a high measure of corroboration ... and may be retained provisionally as the best available theory"; corroboration counts only as "the positive result of a genuinely 'risky' prediction, which might conceivably have been false."
   Anchor: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Karl Popper" (plato.stanford.edu/entries/popper/), sections on demarcation and corroboration.
   Paraphrase: No number of confirming instances can prove a universal law, but one counter-instance can refute it — so corroboration (surviving risky tests) is the most a theory can earn, and only provisionally.

METHOD
Popper analyses the logic of empirical science deductively rather than inductively: a scientist proposes a hypothesis, deduces testable singular predictions from it, and confronts those predictions with observation and experiment. Falsification of a deduced prediction falsifies the theory, whereas a non-falsifying ("verified") outcome only provisionally sustains it. The whole programme replaces the demand for empirical verification/induction with the demand for empirical falsifiability and, for surviving theories, corroboration.

CONTRIBUTION
Cited here as the primary authoritative source for the FALSIFICATIONIST STANCE the manuscript attributes to Popper: scientific theories can never be verified/proven true, only tested by attempts to refute them, and trust/"corroboration" is earned by surviving severe tests (attempts at falsification). The verbatim §3 passages directly state both halves of the claim — that a positive test result "can only temporarily support the theory," that theories are never established as "true" or "probable," and that a theory which "withstands detailed and severe tests" is thereby "corroborated." The §6 demarcation passage supplies falsifiability-not-verifiability as the criterion of science, and the SEP corroborates the verification/falsification asymmetry and the "risky prediction" reading of corroboration. Verdict: SUPPORTED.
