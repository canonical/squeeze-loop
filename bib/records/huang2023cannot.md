METHOD (three sentences)
The authors evaluate GPT-3.5-Turbo (0613) and GPT-4 on GSM8K (1,319 math problems), CommonSenseQA (1,221 MCQs), and HotpotQA (100 multi-hop QA), using a three-step prompt (generate, review/critique, answer again) with NO ground-truth feedback ("intrinsic" self-correction). They contrast this with an "oracle" setting that uses ground-truth labels to decide when to revise, which does improve results (e.g. 84.3% vs 75.9% GSM8K GPT-3.5), showing prior gains depended on the oracle. They report intrinsic self-correction reduces accuracy on all benchmarks (e.g. GPT-4 GSM8K 95.5% -> 91.5% -> 89.0% over rounds; CommonSenseQA GPT-3.5 75.8% -> 38.1%).
LIMITATIONS (authors' own)
The authors acknowledge (§7) their study, though spanning varied strategies/prompts/tasks, may not have found a specific prompt or strategy that could help particular benchmarks; they note that searching for such prompts may inadvertently rely on external feedback and could equally be applied to pre-hoc prompts; GPT-4 was evaluated on a reduced subset (200/dataset, 100 for HotpotQA) to save cost.
CONTRIBUTION (why this paper is cited here; which manuscript claim it supports; verdict)
Cited to support "LLMs cannot yet self-correct reasoning without external feedback, and that self-correction can degrade performance." Body confirms both: the intrinsic (no-feedback) setting is explicitly defined, and degradation is shown numerically on all three reasoning benchmarks (notably the large CommonSenseQA GPT-3.5 drop, plus GSM8K and HotpotQA). VERDICT: SUPPORTED. Both halves are body-verified with quantified degradation; the manuscript phrasing is accurate and not overclaimed.
