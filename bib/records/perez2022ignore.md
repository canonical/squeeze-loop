METHOD (three sentences)
The authors propose PromptInject, a mask-based iterative framework that composes adversarial prompts by inserting attack strings into a base/application prompt template. They run goal-hijacking and prompt-leaking attacks against GPT-3 (text-davinci-002) across a range of handcrafted "rogue strings" and delimiter/escape variations, measuring attack success rate. They show that simple, low-effort inputs achieve high success and that the model's stochasticity yields long-tail residual risk even when most attempts fail.
LIMITATIONS
Evaluation is on GPT-3 (text-davinci-002) only and predates RLHF-hardened frontier models, so absolute success rates may not transfer; attacks are direct user-supplied (first-party) injections rather than indirect/retrieved content; the study targets text-emission goals (printing strings) rather than action-taking/tool-calling agents, so the money-out action class is analogous but not directly measured here.
CONTRIBUTION (why cited in THIS paper; which claim it supports; verdict)
Cited as the canonical demonstration of the prompt-injection / goal-hijacking attack class that Use Case B's authored refund policy must withstand: an adversarial conversational user trying to make the bot "ignore" its policy and commit a refund. It supports the manuscript claim that in-band user instructions can subvert an LLM application's authored intent, motivating why the squeeze loop must enforce the policy externally (independent exerciser + REST action lockpoint) rather than trust the model to honor its own prompt. VERDICT: SUPPORTED. The goal-hijacking/prompt-leaking definitions and the "ignore previous instructions" attack pattern are verbatim-verified and map directly onto the refund-bot manipulation threat.
